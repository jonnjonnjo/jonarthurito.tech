One of the oldest questions in philosophy: do we truly make choices, or are we simply biological machines executing predetermined sequences of cause and effect?

## Determinism

The determinist position holds that every event — including every human decision — is the inevitable result of prior causes. If you knew the position and velocity of every particle in the universe, you could predict every future event, including every "choice" you will ever make.

$$
P(t) = P(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t} v(\tau) \, d\tau
$$

Under strict determinism, the feeling of choosing is an illusion — a story we tell ourselves after the fact.

## Compatibilism

Compatibilists argue that free will and determinism are not mutually exclusive. What matters is not whether your actions are causally determined, but whether they flow from *your own* desires and reasoning, free from external coercion.

> "A man who is compelled by chains is not free. A man who acts from his own character is free — even if that character was shaped by prior causes."

## My Take

I find myself drawn to compatibilism, but with unease. The self that "chooses" is itself a product of genetics and environment. At what point does causation become coercion?

Perhaps the question isn't whether free will exists, but whether the *concept* is even coherent.
